For our reinforcement learning class at Duke, we spent a few months trying to get an
agent to play [Balatro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balatro). I had recently watched The Thinking game
and was convinced (still am) that I'm just like Demis. This is a writeup about what we built and learned along the way.

## Building a simulator

Most previous attempts trained on the actual game through existing mods. That was
painfully slow for the number of rollouts we wanted. So we wrote our own simulator in
Rust and hooked it up to Python. The training side is a normal gym-style reset and step.
The agent gets a 759-dimensional observation vector and a mask over 135 discrete actions
each turn.

The scope for now is the red deck on white stake. The goal is to beat the ante 8 showdown
blind. Here's what we actually built in `balatro-core`:

- The real Balatro RNG. Keyed streams, Tausworthe 223, the same pseudohash and pseudoshuffle
  logic as the Lua game. Give it a real seed string and you get the same boss blinds, dealt
  hands, shop contents, and tags as Steam.
- The full scoring pipeline. All 150 jokers, poker hand detection, chips times mult,
  recursive triggers, held-in-hand bonuses.
- 135 actions with select-and-commit. You toggle cards, then play or discard. The shop has
  buy, sell, reroll, pack select, blind select, joker reordering, even adjacent hand swaps.
  We did not enumerate every possible card subset as its own action.

The observation is a flat vector of the whole game state. Your hand, the full deck with
where each card lives, jokers, consumables, shop slots, voucher flags, chip targets, and
a couple idol forecast scalars at the end. It's a lot, but it's all there.

Verification ended up mattering as much as the sim itself. Maybe more. If the
simulator drifts from the live client, then the agent will learn garbage.

We started by compiling to WASM and running the same actions side by side in a browser tool
and the real game. That caught obvious bugs but it didn't scale. The web app we use now
talks to the Rust sim over HTTP instead. Same idea, just not in the browser anymore.

![The original Balatro game on the left and our web app on the right.](/images/posts/learning-balatro-with-rl/game-vs-webapp.png)

*Figure 1: The real game on the left, our web app on the right.*

Then we hooked into BalatroBot. You step through a run in the sim, replay the same actions
in the live client, and compare state.

We also built a verification system in the cloud around this. When something diverges we
save the full trace to Google Drive and log it in a sheet. That means a trace id, the seed,
the step count, and every action up to the mismatch. We can replay a bug later with
`replay-by-id` and get the exact same sequence. The screenshot below is our fix list. Stuff
like Chaos Joker reroll pricing being wrong, or Four Fingers scoring differently than the
real game. A lot of these look fine on screen and only show up when you diff the actual
state.

![Fix list from our cloud verification system showing saved traces and bug reproduction details.](/images/posts/learning-balatro-with-rl/verification-fixlist.png)

*Figure 2: Our fix list. Each row is a saved trace we could replay to reproduce a bug.*

The next step is to fuzz properly. The idea is to take a decent trained policy, let it
play long rollouts in the sim, mirror those same trajectories in the real game, and diff
the states automatically. Manual clicking got us here. A policy that actually explores
the game should hit the weird joker interactions we haven't found yet.

## Rewards

The core reward lives in Rust and it's pretty bare. Almost everything is zero. You get +1
for clearing a blind. If you play a hand and still have hands left, you get chips scored
divided by the blind target, capped at 1. If you run out of hands without clearing, the
episode ends at -1. Shop moves, discards, rerolls are all zero.

All the interesting shaping lives in Python on top. We have a dense reward scheme with
separate knobs per agent. The blind agent gets nudges for hand type quality, discard credit,
urgency when you're low on hands, and a softer penalty for dying late in a run. The shop
agent gets penalized for crossing interest tier boundaries and rewarded for picking up
jokers. When a blind clears, the shop agent gets credit for the cash and the round win.
When you win a run, both agents get the bonus. We could turn any of this off without
touching Rust.

## Two agents instead of one

We tried a single agent first. It kept getting stuck in one of two modes. Either it would
play hands too aggressively and ignore the economy, or it would hoard money and never buy
jokers. Same network, two totally different skills.

Blind play is short. You see a hand, pick cards, commit. Shop play feels different. You're
spending money and thinking about synergies and planning ahead. So we split it into two
RLlib policies, `blind_agent` and `shop_agent`. Rust projects the game state into a
different observation for each one. The blind agent acts during hand selection. The shop
agent acts in the shop, during pack opening, and even at blind select. Cash out
auto-advances without an agent step.

The single-agent baseline with the same setup couldn't reliably get past the first blind.

![Multi-agent architecture with shared card encoder and separate blind and shop policies.](/images/posts/learning-balatro-with-rl/architecture.png)

*Figure 3: Shared card encoder, separate blind and shop heads.*

I'm not going to dump the full architecture here. The blind policy doesn't pick from 135
actions directly. It uses an autoregressive head that picks play or discard, then toggles
cards one at a time until it commits. We called it choose-or-stop. A flat softmax over
every combination was unstable.

The shop policy uses a shared card encoder over every item on the board. Shop slots,
jokers, pack contents, hand cards. Each token gets a tag for buy, sell, use, or move.
To buy something specific we do a dot product between an intent vector and that card's
embedding. Reroll, end shop, and skip blind got their own heads. We tried treating those
like regular shop items and it didn't work.

The shop entropy had to decay over training. Otherwise it would reroll forever or leave
the shop immediately within a few hundred iterations.

We trained both agents jointly with Ray RLlib multi-agent PPO. We batched hundreds of
games stepping in parallel through the Rust vec env. Our main local run used four env
runners on an RTX A5000, about 20.5M environment steps over 4,000 PPO iterations. On the
cluster we pushed it to 32 runners with 32 envs each.

## What the curves look like

![Five training metrics over the first 1,000 PPO iterations.](/images/posts/learning-balatro-with-rl/training-curves.png)

*Figure 4: Return, first-blind clear rate, episode length, money, and max ante.
Smoothed with a 40-iteration rolling mean.*

For the first ~150 iterations the agent basically dies at the first blind. The return
was near zero. Episodes were short. Money stayed at $4.

Then around iteration 150 everything shifts at once. The first-blind clear rate goes from
near zero to ~99% within about twenty iterations. The return jumps. Episodes get a lot
longer because you're actually reaching the shop with money. The mean max ante climbs
toward 2 and then plateaus around 2.6 to 3.

We're not winning runs. A full run is eight antes and we top out around ante 3 on
average. The variance after that is mostly shop RNG and different joker paths. The blind
policy isn't really getting better at that point. We only plotted the first 1,000 of
4,000 iterations because that's where the interesting part is.

## Takeaways

This was hard. Beating Balatro with RL still feels like an open problem to me, and we
didn't crack it. We got somewhere. Clearing the first blind reliably was a real milestone,
but nowhere close to a winning run. That's fine. The class project was never going to be
the final answer.

Still, it was a blast. Ethan, Ajay, Anjaney, and I spent months on this together, and our
professor liked the project enough that it felt like a real win at the end of the day. W
team.

Ethan and I haven't dropped it. The part that still feels unsolved is hand play, not the
shop or the economy, but actually knowing when to fish for a better hand instead of
taking what's in front of you. We're working on that now: teaching the agent to win by
chasing stronger poker hands, flushes and straights especially, rather than playing the
first decent thing it sees. It's the same problem in a different form, and we're still
early.

I'll probably write another piece about that when we have something worth showing. For now
this is where the class project landed.
